The Flexible Liturgy Tool

How the bulletin maker becomes the tool liturgical churches reach for when they switch settings, follow a different calendar, or write their own liturgy — without becoming a design program.

July 20, 2026 · Synthesized from 8 research inputs: codebase audit, liturgical-tradition domain research, 11-bulletin field corpus, competitive landscape, provider ToS/API posture, open-data audit, CCLI/OneLicense precedent, DIY-workflow evidence.

1 · The thesis

Every incumbent in this market stops one step before the finish line. Sundays & Seasons and Lutheran Service Builder — the two strongest denominational tools — both end, by their own documentation, at "export to Word and format your bulletin yourself." Planning Center prints an unstyled report dump. Proclaim and ProPresenter are screens-only by design. Two paid scheduling tools produce no document at all. Nobody ships a finished, professionally typeset, print-ready worship folder as the product's terminal output. We already do — for exactly one church's liturgy.

The generalization opportunity is therefore not "add customization." It is: keep the finish line we already own, and make the path to it configurable along the four axes churches actually vary on — order of service, texts, content source, calendar — while refusing, as a matter of product identity, to vary the axis they don't (presentation). The pastor designs the rite once; the volunteer's Wednesday afternoon stays exactly as simple as it is today.

Positioning sentence. The tool that turns your liturgy — whatever it is, from whatever tradition, on whatever calendar — into a beautiful printed worship folder your congregation can follow, in minutes a week, by a volunteer.

2 · What the research established

From 11 real bulletins across six traditions

  • Within-tradition structural variance is startlingly low. Two ELCA churches with opposite personalities ran the same ~15-element skeleton; LCMS bulletins are literally software-generated from one shared skeleton; both UMC samples follow the denomination's published pattern element-for-element. The big between-tradition seam is simply whether communion happens weekly.
  • ~10 starter templates + pluggable occasion modules (baptism, confirmation, bishop's visit, Advent candles) credibly cover the large majority of American liturgical practice.
  • The block vocabulary is small and closed. Heading, rubric, dialogue, hymn (ref / lyrics / notation), reading (ref / full), responsive psalm, prayer, freeform — plus a handful of non-liturgical types (prayer list, calendar, serving roster).
  • Import is tractable. None of the 11 PDFs were scans; liturgy sections are single-column with a closed heading vocabulary and regex-able P:/C: dialogue. "Upload last week's bulletin, we draft your rite" is a real onboarding feature, not a moonshot.
  • Non-liturgical content is 30–75% of real bulletins — the one scope question this research forces (§8).

From the domain research

  • Every tradition surveyed (ELCA, LCMS, Episcopal, Catholic, UMC, PC(USA), free-church) maps onto the same four configurable axes. Traditions differ enormously in content and vocabulary, far less in shape.
  • Settings are a stable menu, not a weekly rotation — churches keep a default plus a seasonal or occasional alternate. Design accordingly: saved rites + per-service picker + optional seasonal rules, never calendar-locked.
  • Role labels are a trap: Lutheran "P:" = Pastor; some Reformed bulletins use "P:" = People. Speaker roles must be semantic (leader / congregation / rubric / sung) with per-church display labels. Our existing DialogRole primitive already models this.
  • The licensing floor is friendlier than feared: the ELLC/ICET common texts — creeds, Lord's Prayer, Gloria, Sanctus, the exact "ordinary" blocks a rite library needs — are public domain. NRSV print use in bulletins is free to 500 verses. Real per-item friction concentrates in hymns (CCLI vs OneLicense, per-song).
  • Alternative calendars are structurally diverse: Catholic numbering differs from RCL for the same readings; LCMS splits between 1-year and 3-year systems; Narrative Lectionary runs Sept–May with one text per week; Season of Creation overlays any base; no-lectionary churches need the engine fully bypassable.

From the codebase audit

  • The expensive part: the ordo lives as ~500 lines of interleaved HTML in two templates kept in sync by convention, guarded by pinned page-count regression tests. Making order-of-service data-driven is the one big rock.
  • Already general: the (DialogRole, text) call-response atom; the text-resolution chain (user override → publisher → static fallback); the bounded-registry pattern proven twice (settings, paper); clean core/UI separation; per-church profile as the sanctioned extension point.
  • Nearly free: ELW settings are ~10 notation images + 2 omissions — the texts don't vary. "Serve churches that switch settings" is cheap; alternative ordos and calendars are the real work.
  • A conflation to unwind: season.py mixes RCL calendar facts with one church's house customs (Lent drops the Kyrie…). These must become separate layers: calendar providers vs. per-church seasonal preferences.

From the open-data audit

  • Venite's Liturgy Document Format (MIT) is direct prior art: a real-world-tested JSON schema whose block types (heading, rubric, responsive, psalm, option, bible-reading) independently match our corpus-derived vocabulary. Its Condition model (gate any block on season/feast/week/preference) solves "how a rite adapts to the day." Its LiturgicalDay carries multiple lectionary-cycle positions at once. The author disavows the code — adopt the schema design, write our own engine.
  • Calendar math is mostly borrowable: Easter via python-dateutil; RCL as consumable data (Vanderbilt ICS/CSV); the romcal rank+override precedence pattern is small to hand-port. LCMS 1-year and Narrative Lectionary are bounded one-time transcriptions no competitor has bothered to do.
  • Scripture licensing maps per tradition: NET Bible push-button safe; ESV free API (LCMS market); NRSV free in print but needs a licensing call for API automation; WEB/KJV public domain floor; NABRE (Catholic) requires paid per-parish USCCB licenses — reinforcing Catholic-out-of-scope.
  • Preservation done: the only structured public-domain hymn-tune corpus (Open Hymnal, ABC notation) survives only in the Wayback Machine; a mirror job is archiving it now.

3 · The four-axis model

Everything a liturgical church might want to customize decomposes into four independent axes. Presentation is deliberately not one of them.

AxisWhat it isWho sets itHow often
Rite (ordo)Which blocks exist, in what order; occasion modulesPastor / musicianRarely — adopted, then stable
Setting / textsWhich fixed texts & music fill the ordinary slotsPastor / musicianSeasonally or per-service, from a saved menu
Calendar / lectionaryWhat resolves a date into day name, propers, readingsChurch, onceAlmost never (plus per-week manual override)
Content sourcePublisher subscription, public-domain library, own texts, pasteChurch adminOnce per linked account

The weekly volunteer touches none of these. Their flow stays: pick date → fetch → choose hymns → generate.

4 · Design concept: rites as data, presentation as house style

A rite is an ordered list of typed blocks, stored as data (Venite-LDF-informed schema), not template HTML:

  • Literal blocks carry the church's own text — a dialogue block is a list of (role, text) lines with semantic roles rendered per the church's label convention (P:/C:, Celebrant/People, bold-only).
  • Slot blocks reference a proper — first_reading, psalm, preface_of_day, hymn:gathering — resolved at generation time by the church's calendar provider + content sources.
  • Conditions gate blocks on season, feast, or a service-level toggle ("communion today?", "baptism?") — replacing today's hardcoded seasonal config with per-church rules layered over calendar facts.
  • Occasion modules (baptism, confirmation, Advent candles, funeral, wedding) are insertable block groups — matching how real bulletins graft special rites onto a base ordo.

The renderer keeps sole ownership of typography, pagination, the auto-tighten system, and the five-document family (bulletin, large print, leader guide, pulpit sheets). Editing a rite means reordering and toggling typed blocks and editing text in structured fields — never fonts, margins, or layout. The no-WYSIWYG stance survives intact because customization is structural and textual, not visual.

Churches start from the library, not from blank. Starter rites per tradition (ELW settings, LSB Divine Services, BCP Rite I/II, UMC pattern, Reformed order), duplicated and tweaked — or drafted automatically by the import wizard from an uploaded Word/PDF bulletin (LLM segmentation over a closed heading vocabulary; human-confirmable draft, never silent).

5 · Three directions & a recommendation

A — Deep Lutheran

Stay ELCA-shaped; add rite flexibility within ELW, then LSB for LCMS. Smallest step, safest content story (we know the S&S integration), immediately serves "switches settings often." Ceiling: two denominations.

B — The Liturgy Engine

The full four-axis architecture, multi-tradition starter library, pluggable calendars and content sources. The durable product; the moat (nobody else has liturgical intelligence and finished print). Risk: building generality before revenue proves each segment.

C — The Print Companion

Position against Planning Center et al.: import their plan, we produce the beautiful print. Cheap wedge, but concedes the liturgical intelligence — and PC's liturgical-church penetration is weak precisely because the plan data we'd import is liturgically empty.

Recommendation: B, built through A, with C as a later import format rather than a strategy. Build the rite engine underneath the current ELCA product with output regression-locked to today's bulletins (Phase 0 changes nothing visible). Every subsequent phase ships a sellable expansion — settings flexibility, own-texts, alternative calendars, LCMS — so generality is financed segment by segment, never speculatively. The Narrative-Lectionary and 1-year-lectionary niches are cheap to serve and completely unserved: perfect early beachheads for exactly the churches the owner named.

6 · The clearinghouse pillar

The owner's framing: link whatever content subscriptions you already hold; we assemble and print from any of them. This is our production S&S model (church-owned credentials, encrypted vault, entitlement-gated cache) generalized to a provider registry. It repositions us from potential publisher-competitor to the neutral assembly layer that makes their subscriptions more valuable.

Operating rules for the permissionless posture (owner-endorsed): every provider fetch uses the church's own paid credentials; the entitlement rule is non-negotiable (content never flows to a church without its own subscription — the line between "integration" and "piracy vector"); fetch politely and cache aggressively so we load their servers less than a human clicking; treat every provider as breakable (cache + graceful degradation + manual paste fallback, all already built); respond cooperatively if contacted. The legally clean floor — public-domain library + manual paste + import — means the product works with zero linked accounts, and every link is additive.

7 · Feature bets, ranked by attractiveness ÷ cost

  1. Finished print (keep owning it). The confirmed market-wide gap; also: large print, leader guide, and pulpit sheets are accessibility features every competitor lacks entirely.
  2. Rite menu & switching. Saved rites, per-service picker, seasonal rules. Cheap (settings are mostly images today) and it's the owner's named segment.
  3. Import-your-bulletin onboarding. Corpus-validated tractable. Converts the scariest moment (data entry) into the magic moment. Also the answer to "what about my weird local liturgy."
  4. Own-texts library. Persistent per-church text bank (today's custom edits die weekly — an explicit UI apology). Low cost: the options-catalog shape already supports N presets.
  5. Alternative calendars. RCL in-house, manual/sermon-series mode, Narrative Lectionary, LCMS 1-year, Season of Creation overlay. Bounded data work; zero competitors.
  6. Public-domain library. ELLC ordinary texts + WEB/KJV + NET API + archived Open Hymnal tunes. The zero-subscription floor.
  7. Licensing helper. Per-hymn license tags (CCLI / OneLicense / PD), auto copyright footers, exportable OneLicense/CCLI usage reports. Converts diffuse anxiety into a checkbox.
  8. Provider expansion (LSB, riteplanning). High value, gated on ToS findings and segment order.

8 · The announcements fork

Non-liturgical content — prayer lists, calendars, serving rosters, birthdays, flyers — is 30–75% of real bulletins by page count in every sampled tradition except Catholic worship aids. Two honest options:

  • Liturgy-only. "We generate your order of service." Real product type (it's exactly how Catholic parishes split documents). Tight scope; but visibly not "your bulletin" for most Protestant churches.
  • Structured announcement blocks. Prayer list, week calendar, serving roster, staff directory, freeform announcement — all typed, listy content observed universally in the corpus. House-styled like everything else; still no WYSIWYG, no image collages, no flyer design.
Lean: the middle path, as a late phase. Once blocks exist, announcement block types are cheap, and they change the sales sentence from "part of your bulletin" to "your whole bulletin." The line to hold: structured lists yes, freeform page design never. Decision needed from the owner before Phase 4 is briefed.

9 · Phased build plan

Each phase is independently shippable, sequenced so revenue-relevant capability lands before speculative generality. Sized in familiar workstream terms; every one decomposes into agent-executable briefs with the existing regression suites as acceptance gates.

LWS-0
(L)
Rite engine under the floorboards. Block schema (LDF-informed); extract the ordo from the two ~500-line templates into one data-driven rite definition the renderer iterates; reproduce current output exactly (pinned layout tests are the acceptance gate, re-baselined only for deliberate changes); split calendar facts from house seasonal customs. No visible change. The one big rock — everything else stands on it.
LWS-1
(S–M)
Texts & settings menu. Persistent per-church text library (fix "custom edits are not saved"); ELW settings 6–10 + rite variants as data; per-service rite picker + per-church seasonal rules UI. First visible win for setting-switchers.
LWS-2
(M)
Rite editor + starter library + occasion modules. Structured block editor (reorder, toggle, edit lines — no layout controls); the ~10-template starter library; baptism/confirmation/Advent-candles/funeral modules; block-level preview.
LWS-3
(M)
Calendar providers. In-house RCL (Vanderbilt data + owned tables); manual/sermon-series mode; Narrative Lectionary + LCMS 1-year transcriptions; Season of Creation overlay; per-week override. Removes the S&S-server dependency for calendar math.
LWS-4
(M–L)
Content-source registry + PD library + import wizard. Provider interface (S&S becomes one of N); ELLC/scripture/PD-hymn library; NET/ESV scripture APIs; upload-and-draft rite import (LLM segmentation, human-confirmed).
LWS-5
(M)
LCMS beachhead. LSB starter rites; ESV default; credential-linked LSB content probe (pending ToS read); hymn file-upload path; onboarding aimed at 1-year-lectionary churches.
LWS-6
(S–M)
Announcements blocks (if the fork resolves that way) + licensing helper (license tags, footers, usage-report export).

Sequencing note: LWS-0 is the gate; 1–2 monetize the owner's named segment; 3 unlocks alternative-calendar churches; 4–5 open the second denomination. WS-9 (deployment) from the platform track can interleave anywhere.

10 · Risks & open questions

  • The LWS-0 refactor is the project's riskiest engineering. Mitigation: regression-locked extraction (same output, byte-similar), the existing layout suite as the gate, phased per-document migration.
  • Scope creep toward WYSIWYG. Every editor decision must pass: "structural or textual, never visual." The moment we expose a font picker we inherit the support burden we designed this product to avoid.
  • Provider ToS exposure. Posture chosen (permissionless + entitlement + politeness); residual risks are provider breakage (engineered for) and church-account suspension (fetch discipline). Final S&S/LSB/riteplanning ToS detail lands with the pending consolidated report.
  • NRSV automation licensing — the one content conversation worth having proactively, since ELCA is home turf and print use is already free.
  • Hobbyist-data fragility — vendor lectionary data in-house (owned tables seeded from Vanderbilt/transcriptions), never live-depend on one-person APIs.
  • Owner decisions needed: segment order after ELCA (LCMS vs Episcopal vs calendar-niche churches first); the announcements fork (§8); pricing posture within the $30–75/mo evidence band; when the Augsburg partnership letter ships.

Prepared as the ideation-phase deliverable. Next step on approval: turn LWS-0 into implementation briefs using the established workstream machinery (contracts, acceptance criteria, guardrails, agent execution with orchestrated review).
